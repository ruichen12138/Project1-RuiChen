Question 1: Is the high price means high rating? How are they related?

When people purchase wine at a supermarket, price oftenly plays a significant role in their decision-making process. However, the question arises: does a higher price mean a better quality of wine?

To solve this problem, we will analyze the relationship between wine ratings and their prices.

Firstly, we can calculate the correlation between these two variables, WineRating and WinePrice, since it is the most obvious method to see if theyre related.

In[2]:
import pandas as pd

file_path = 'white-wine-price-rating.csv' 

wine_data = pd.read_csv(file_path)

from scipy import stats

x = wine_data['WineRating']  
y = wine_data['WinePrice']

slope, intercept, r_value, p_value, std_err = stats.linregress(x, y)

print(f"Correlation Coefficient: {r_value}")

Correlation Coefficient: 0.3387388744417564

We can see the correlation for this dataset is only 0.34, indicating that there is a slightly positive relationship.

Also, we can conduct a linear regression on this dataset, taking the price as independent variable and the rating as dependent variable.

In[3]:
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

predicted_y = intercept + slope * x

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))

plt.scatter(x, y, alpha=0.5, label='Actual WinePrice')

plt.plot(x, predicted_y, color='red', label='Predicted WinePrice')

plt.title('Linear Regression: WineRating vs. WinePrice')
plt.xlabel('WineRating')
plt.ylabel('WinePrice ($)')
plt.legend()

plt.show()

The graph shows a positive correlation between price and rating, particularly for ratings below 4.6. However, for wines with ratings above 4.6, the price variability increases significantly, becoming more erratic. This variability in higher price ranges contributes to a decrease in the overall correlation between price and rating.

The correlation coefficient between wine price and rating is 0.34, which means they have a positive, but not very strong connection. So, a high price doesn't guarantee a top-rated wine, but the price can sometimes point us to a good wine. If we are buying a wine, other factors also need to think about, like brand, availability and so on. Therefore, we might find excellent wines that aren't pricey and some expensive ones that don't live up to their cost. When shopping for wine, please don't just focus on the price, but also consider other qualities too.

Question 3: For high rating wines, what is common words they used in their name?

We choose the wine rating from the 4.6-5.0, which I think its a fair range to consider as a good wine. In order to find the common words, we first need to separate the full name of wine into words and count the number of each word. Thus, I made the following table showing the first 9 common words:

In[6]:
from collections import Counter
import re

high_rating_wines = wine_data[(wine_data['WineRating'] >= 4.6) & (wine_data['WineRating'] <= 5.0)]

full_names = high_rating_wines['FullName']

def tokenize(name):
    # Convert to lowercase and remove non-alphabetic characters
    tokens = re.findall(r'\b[a-z]+\b', name.lower())
    return tokens

all_words = [word for name in full_names for word in tokenize(name)]

word_counts = Counter(all_words)

most_common_words = word_counts.most_common(10) 

most_common_words_df = pd.DataFrame(most_common_words, columns=['Word', 'Frequency'])

display(most_common_words_df)

Conclusion

Next time, if we want to find some good wines, we can seek assistance from the following methods:

  1. We can consider the price of the wine. Although more expensive wines do not necessarily mean they have better reviews, wines that are priced higher generally receive more favorable ratings.
  2. When purchasing wine, we can check if the region of origin is 'Chilean,' which could be an indicator of good rating.
  3. While looking at the names of wines, we can observe whether they include words like 'Domaine,' 'Montrachet,' 'cru,' and 'grand,' because words are frequently found in high-rated wines.
